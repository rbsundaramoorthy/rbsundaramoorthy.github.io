My grandmother measured spices with her palm. My culinary school instructor measured them with a digital scale accurate to 0.1 grams. For years, I thought one of them had to be wrong. It took me an embarrassingly long time to realize they were both right — they were just solving different problems.

Palm-measured cooking is about intuition, adaptation, and the understanding that today's tomatoes are not yesterday's tomatoes. Scale-measured cooking is about reproducibility, precision, and the confidence that the dish will taste the same in Tokyo as it does in Chennai. Neither approach is superior. They're different tools for different contexts, and the best cooks I know switch between them without thinking.

I've started to see this pattern everywhere. In engineering, there's the "move fast and break things" school and the "measure twice, cut once" school. In design, there's intuition-driven work and data-driven work. The people who do the most interesting work aren't the ones who pick a side — they're the ones who can hold both approaches in their head simultaneously and reach for the right one at the right moment.

The kitchen taught me that contradiction isn't confusion. It's capacity.
